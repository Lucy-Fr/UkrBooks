Kateryna Kalytko

Introduction

Kateryna Kalytko is a Ukrainian poet, prose writer, and translator whose work brings together lyrical intensity, ethical pressure, and a sustained attention to violence, displacement, and the fragility of human ties. She is also one of the key Ukrainian translators of contemporary Bosnian, Croatian, and Serbian literature into Ukrainian. Her writing often works through contrast: beauty turning into shock, the everyday turning into evidence, and private feeling becoming a register of collective catastrophe.

Biography

Kalytko was born on 8 March 1982 in Vinnytsia. She completed secondary school there with a gold medal and studied political science and journalism at the National University of Kyiv-Mohyla Academy (1999–2005). She lives and works between Vinnytsia and Sarajevo.

In 2017 her book Zemlia Zahublenykh, abo Malenki strashni kazky (The Land of the Lost, or Little Terrible Tales) received the BBC Ukrainian Book of the Year award. In 2019 she received the independent Women in Arts award (UN Women Ukraine and the Ukrainian Institute, HeForShe Arts Week). In 2023 she was awarded the Taras Shevchenko National Prize (Literature) for the poetry collection Orden movchalnyts (Order of the Silent Women). In the same year she was invited to write the text for the Radio Dictation of National Unity.

Writing

Kalytko publishes poetry and prose; her texts have appeared in Ukrainian periodicals and literary journals. She is known for compact, high-density poetic narratives where metaphor does not decorate experience but compresses it into a form that can hold fear, loss, and moral ambiguity. A recurrent motif is the search for “home” as a precarious construct—geographical, bodily, and linguistic—under conditions of historical violence and war.

Translation and Cultural Work

Kalytko researches and translates contemporary literature from Bosnia and Herzegovina and the wider South Slavic region. Her translated cycles and projects have introduced Ukrainian readers to a range of Balkan authors (including Miljenko Jergović, Semezdin Mehmedinović, Lamija Begagić, and others). In December 2017 the independent publisher “Kruhovert” released Meša Selimović’s cult novel Dervish and Death in her Ukrainian translation.

Interview and Critical Context

In a long conversation with LB.ua (2019), Kalytko speaks about independence as a professional and ethical stance, about her conscious choice of Ukrainian as a language of belonging, and about Bosnia as a lens for thinking through the mechanisms of war, fragmentation, and the work of rebuilding social ties. She also outlines her view of war literature as a process that unfolds in stages (from immediate documentation to later, harsher reflection) and discusses a planned novel set against the background of World War I and the Ukrainian struggles of 1914–1924.
